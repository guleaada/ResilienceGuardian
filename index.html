❓ How Resilience Guardian Works

AI-powered crop diagnosis grounded in peer-reviewed Ethiopian agricultural research — available in 4 languages.

6 Steps to a Diagnosis

1

Select your crop

Choose from the three crops the app specialises in — Enset (እንሰት), Teff (ጤፍ), or Wheat (ስንዴ). Each crop has its own disease knowledge base built from Ethiopian research.

2

Upload a photo (optional but recommended)

Take a photo of the affected leaf, stem, corm, or any part of the plant. The AI visually inspects the image for disease signs — discolouration, lesions, ooze, pest damage, rust patterns.

3

Select visible symptoms

Tap the symptoms you observe on your plant. Each crop shows relevant symptoms — for example Enset shows "heart-leaf wilting first" which is the key early sign of Bacterial Wilt (Xcm).

4

Add context about your farm

Describe recent rainfall, your location zone, whether you used the same machete on multiple plants, or if a neighbour's farm is also affected. This context significantly improves diagnosis accuracy.

5

Choose your language

Select English, አማርኛ (Amharic), Afaan Oromo, or ትግርኛ (Tigrinya). The AI responds in your chosen language so advice is accessible to any farmer or extension worker.

6

Get research-backed advice

The AI cross-references your photo and symptoms against a knowledge base built from 8 peer-reviewed Ethiopian research papers. It returns: disease diagnosis, confidence score, severity level, numbered action steps, traditional indigenous knowledge, local language summary, and the specific research source used.

What the AI Result Includes

🔴

Severity badge

High / Moderate / Low risk — tells the farmer how urgently to act.

📋

Numbered action steps

Step-by-step practical actions ordered by priority — most critical first (e.g. "Clean your machete with fire or bleach BEFORE moving to the next plant").

🌿

Traditional knowledge

Indigenous Ethiopian farming practices for the specific problem — citing the region or community where the practice is documented.

📚

Research source

Every response cites the specific paper or institution whose research was used — full transparency on where the advice comes from.

📞

Nearest help

Direct tap-to-call links to the nearest agricultural extension office for your crop — so farmers can get expert human support immediately.

Every piece of advice in this app is grounded in real peer-reviewed Ethiopian agricultural research — not generic global databases.

🌱 Enset Research — 5 Papers

🍌 Integrated Management of Enset Bacterial Wilt
Negash T. et al. — Bonga University, 2023

Comprehensive study on Xanthomonas campestris pv. musacearum (Xcm) management. Documents transmission routes, symptom progression, survival times (3 months in soil, 3–4 days on knives), and management strategies. Up to 80% of Ethiopian enset farms infected.

👥 Community-Based Integrated EBW Management
Temam B., Getahun M., Kebede M., Tsegaye Y. — Werabe ARC / Central Ethiopia ARI, 2023

Field trial in Cheha District (Gurage) and Mirab Azernet (Siltie), 2019–2021. Before intervention: 65.7% disease prevalence. After community-based management with 163 trained stakeholders and simultaneous mass rouging: reduced to 10.1% in Cheha and 5.6% in Mirab Azernet.

🤖 Enset Disease & Pest Identification Using CNN
Tsegaye Yibgeta Biza — Jimma University, 2021

First deep learning system for Ethiopian enset disease identification. Achieved 98.87% test accuracy classifying Healthy / Bacterial Wilt / Root Mealybug. Data collected with experts from Gurage Zone Agricultural Institute and Wolkite University. Also identified Sigatoka, leaf speckle, and Cordana as visually distinct diseases.

🌾 Teff Research — 2 Papers

🌱 Major Tef Diseases in Ethiopia and their Management
Degete A.G. — EIAR Debre Zeit Agricultural Research Center, 2021

Comprehensive guide to 5 teff diseases: Leaf Rust (10–41% yield loss), Head Smudge, Zonate Eye Spot, Smut, and Damping Off. Includes symptom descriptions, host range, management recommendations and fungicide options.

Field survey of 35 teff fields across West Shewa, South-West Shewa, and North Shewa zones. Highest leaf rust severity (90%) recorded in Mendida district. Zero rust in Woliso/Dendi. Head smudge worst in Liben Jawe (West Shewa), absent in North Shewa.

🌿 Wheat Research — 1 Paper

🌻 Early Warning System for Wheat Rust in Ethiopia
Allen-Sader C. et al. — Cambridge / Met Office / CIMMYT / EIAR, 2019

Documents Ethiopia's weather-based spore dispersal early warning system for wheat rusts. Rust spores travel hundreds to thousands of km on wind — can originate from Yemen or circulate within Ethiopian highlands. Critical monitoring period: tillering to heading. Differentiates stripe rust (10–15°C), stem rust Ug99, leaf rust, and septoria.

How Research Becomes Advice

Each paper was read and converted into a structured knowledge base. When you submit a query, the AI receives the full knowledge base for your crop and is instructed to base its advice strictly on those research findings — citing the specific paper used. This means advice like "disinfect your machete with fire or bleach before moving to the next plant" comes directly from Negash et al. 2023, not from generic internet data.

The knowledge base includes: disease names (scientific and local), symptom descriptions in sequence, transmission routes ranked by importance, management steps ranked by effectiveness, proven real-world results, traditional indigenous practices with regional attribution, and climate resilience notes.

Why This Was Built

Existing crop disease apps like Plantix, Nuru, and Tumaini are powerful tools — but they are built for global or sub-Saharan general crops. None of them focus on Enset, Ethiopia's most important food security crop for 20+ million people. None give advice in Afaan Oromo or Tigrinya. None use research from Ethiopian institutions like EIAR, Jimma University, Bonga University, or Werabe Agricultural Research Centre.

Resilience Guardian fills that gap — built by Gulilat Kasiye Worku, an Ethiopian developer, for Ethiopian farmers, using Ethiopian science.

What Makes It Different

Local research: Every piece of advice cites a real Ethiopian paper — not a global dataset. The app knows that the highest teff rust severity is in Mendida (North Shewa), that community rouging in Siltie Zone reduced disease from 65.7% to 5.6%, and that contaminated machetes are the #1 way Enset Bacterial Wilt spreads.

4 Ethiopian languages: Full advice in English, አማርኛ, Afaan Oromo, and ትግርኛ — so the app is useful from Kaffa to Tigray.

Image analysis: Upload a photo of your plant and the AI visually inspects it for disease signs alongside your reported symptoms.
